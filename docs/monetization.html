Game Theory Monetization Engine

Lua Implemented in Lua, executed via wasmoon in the browser.

The economy engine uses four behavioral economics principles to maximize conversion and ARPU (Average Revenue Per User).

1. Decoy Pricing

The Decoy Effect: When presented with three options, users tend to compare similar ones. The Pro tier is priced to make the Ultimate tier look like an incredible deal.

Why it works: The Pro tier (1,800) and Ultimate tier (2,000) are only 200 coins apart, but the Pro tier's value_mult (2.8x) is much worse than Ultimate's (5.0x). Users perceive Ultimate as the "smart choice" and upgrade.

2. Endowed Progress Effect

Endowed Progress: Users start with 2 of 10 battle pass stamps already completed. This leverages the Goal Gradient Effect — people work harder toward a goal the closer they get to completing it.

Implementation: get_endowed_progress(user_id) returns the current stamp count. New users start at 2/10 (20%). Purchasing tiers awards additional stamps:

  • Basic: +0 stamps
  • Pro: +1 stamp (now at 3/10 = 30%)
  • Ultimate: +3 stamps (now at 5/10 = 50%)

3. Sunk Cost Fallacy (Dynamic Discounting)

Sunk Cost: Players who have invested significant playtime receive a price discount. The more they've played, the more they've "invested" — and the more likely they are to convert.

4. Loss Aversion (Upsell Modal)

Loss Aversion: Users feel losses more acutely than equivalent gains. After purchasing Pro, the upsell modal highlights how little extra they'd pay for Ultimate.

When a user buys the Pro (Decoy) tier, the Lua engine sets show_upsell = true. The JS UI displays a modal emphasizing the marginal cost difference (only +200 coins), making the upgrade feel urgent.
